The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks

Author: Rebecca Skloot

About the Book

The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks by Rebecca Skloot, published in 2010, tells the extraordinary true story of Henrietta Lacks, an African American woman whose cells, taken without her knowledge in 1951, became one of the most important tools in medicine. Known as HeLa, her cells have contributed to countless scientific breakthroughs, including the development of the polio vaccine, cancer research, and in vitro fertilization. The book intertwines the scientific, ethical, and personal aspects of Henrietta‚Äôs story, shedding light on issues of race, consent, and medical ethics.


Plot Summary

The narrative alternates between the scientific achievements made possible by HeLa cells and the personal history of Henrietta Lacks and her family.

Henrietta, a poor tobacco farmer from Virginia, sought treatment at Johns Hopkins Hospital for cervical cancer. Without her knowledge or consent, a sample of her cancerous cells was taken during treatment. These cells were the first to survive and reproduce indefinitely in a lab, making them "immortal."

Through this journey, the book explores the human cost of scientific progress and the ethical implications of using human tissue in research without informed consent.


Why Read This Book?

This book is a compelling mix of biography, scientific history, and ethical investigation. It brings humanity to the story of medical research and highlights the often-overlooked individuals behind scientific progress.
