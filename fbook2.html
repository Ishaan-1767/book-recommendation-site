1984

Author: George Orwell

About the Book

1984 is a dystopian novel by George Orwell, published in 1949. Set in a totalitarian future society, it is a chilling portrayal of surveillance, propaganda, and state control. The novel's themes of government overreach, loss of individuality, and the manipulation of truth have made it a cornerstone of modern literature and a timeless warning about the dangers of authoritarianism. Its concepts, like Big Brother and doublethink, have become cultural touchstones and remain relevant to this day.


Plot Summary

The story is set in Airstrip One (formerly Great Britain), a province of the superstate Oceania, perpetually at war with either Eurasia or Eastasia. The Party, led by the figurehead Big Brother, exercises absolute control over every aspect of life, including thought, history, and language. Through mechanisms like the Thought Police and constant surveillance, individuality and dissent are ruthlessly eradicated.

The protagonist, Winston Smith, works at the Ministry of Truth, where he alters historical records to align with the Party's shifting narratives.
