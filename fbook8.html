Moby-Dick

Author: Herman Melville

About the Book

Moby-Dick, or The Whale, is a novel by Herman Melville, first published in 1851. Often regarded as one of the greatest works of American literature, it is an epic tale of obsession, revenge, and the relationship between humanity and nature. The book combines elements of adventure, philosophy, and metaphysics, creating a richly layered narrative that explores complex themes like fate, free will, and the limits of human understanding.


Plot Summary

The story is narrated by Ishmael, a sailor who joins the crew of the whaling ship Pequod, captained by the enigmatic and monomaniacal Ahab. Ahab is consumed by his quest for vengeance against Moby Dick, a massive white whale that had previously maimed him by biting off his leg.

The journey of the Pequod takes the crew across vast and treacherous oceans, encountering exotic locales, dangerous seas, and other whaling ships. Along the way, Ishmael introduces the reader to the culture, industry, and dangers of 19th-century whaling, as well as philosophical musings on existence, religion, and humanity's place in the universe.

As the ship closes in on Moby Dick, the narrative becomes increasingly intense. The climactic battle between Ahab and the whale is both thrilling and tragic, ending with the destruction of the Pequod and the death of nearly all its crew. Ishmael survives, floating on a coffin-turned-lifeboat, to tell the tale.
